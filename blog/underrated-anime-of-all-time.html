Top 10 Underrated Anime of All Time

By AniCrunch Editorial · 12 min read

The anime community has a frustrating habit of recycling the same recommendations. Ask for something new and you'll get pointed toward the same dozen titles that dominate every "best of" list. But beneath the surface of mainstream visibility lies a vast landscape of exceptional anime that never found their audience—not because they lacked quality, but because they lacked timing, marketing, or viral moments.

These aren't obscure titles included just for the sake of being contrarian. Every entry on this list represents genuine artistic achievement, emotional depth, or creative ambition that went largely unnoticed. Some were overshadowed by bigger releases in the same season. Others were simply too niche, too experimental, or too ahead of their time. What they share is a level of craft that deserves to be celebrated, discussed, and discovered by a wider audience. These are the hidden gems we wish more people talked about.

10. Shouwa Genroku Rakugo Shinjuu

A period drama about the dying art of rakugo (traditional Japanese comedic storytelling) doesn't exactly scream "mass appeal," which is precisely why this masterpiece flew under so many radars. The series follows multiple generations of rakugo performers, weaving together themes of artistic legacy, obsession, jealousy, and the tension between preserving tradition and embracing change.

What makes Rakugo Shinjuu extraordinary is its refusal to compromise. The rakugo performances themselves are delivered in full, with characters performing complete stories-within-stories that illuminate their psychology and relationships. The animation cleverly shifts style during performances, reflecting each performer's unique approach. It's a slow burn that rewards patience with some of the most mature, nuanced character work in anime. The relationship between Yakumo and Sukeroku forms the emotional core—a bond that's equal parts friendship, rivalry, and something harder to define. This is anime for adults in the truest sense: thoughtful, melancholic, and unafraid of silence.

9. Odd Taxi

On the surface, Odd Taxi appears to be a quirky slice-of-life about anthropomorphic animals driving taxis. That description does the series a profound disservice. What starts as seemingly disconnected vignettes gradually reveals itself as an intricately plotted mystery where every conversation, every passenger, and every throwaway detail connects to a larger conspiracy involving a missing girl, social media obsession, and the dark underbelly of Tokyo nightlife.

The brilliance lies in the execution. The animal character designs initially feel like a gimmick until you realize they're a deliberate stylistic choice that lets the show explore heavy themes without feeling oppressively grim. The dialogue is sharp, naturalistic, and often darkly funny. The pacing is masterful—each episode drops new puzzle pieces that retroactively recontextualize earlier scenes. By the finale, what seemed like a relaxed character study transforms into a taut thriller with genuine stakes. Odd Taxi proves that original anime can still surprise us, but it came and went in 2021 with far less fanfare than it deserved.

8. Paranoia Agent

Satoshi Kon's only television series remains criminally underwatched despite his reputation as one of anime's greatest auteurs. Paranoia Agent tackles social anxiety, collective delusion, and the ways societies create scapegoats to avoid confronting deeper problems. The setup—a mysterious attacker on golden rollerblades assaulting people across Tokyo—is merely the entry point into a surreal, psychological exploration of modern Japanese culture.

Each episode functions as a standalone vignette focusing on different characters, all connected by the enigmatic Lil' Slugger. Kon blurs the line between reality and fantasy so thoroughly that by the midpoint, you're questioning what's actually happening versus what's collective hallucination. The show tackles heavy subjects—suicide, PTSD, celebrity culture, the pressure to conform—with a visual inventiveness that only Kon could deliver. Some episodes are terrifying, others are darkly comedic, and a few are genuinely heartbreaking. It's challenging, occasionally oblique, and demands active engagement. That's exactly why it's essential viewing that too few people have experienced.

7. Planetes

Hard science fiction is rare in anime, which makes Planetes feel like a minor miracle. Set in the near future where space travel is routine but unglamorous, the series follows debris collectors—basically garbage workers in orbit—as they navigate both the practical hazards of space work and the political tensions of an expanding space economy. It's grounded, methodical, and refreshingly realistic in its portrayal of space travel.

What elevates Planetes beyond its premise is the character work. Protagonist Hachimaki starts as an ambitious dreamer who wants to own his own spaceship, but the series methodically deconstructs his individualistic ideology through encounters with terrorism, corporate exploitation, and the human cost of space colonization. The show explores class struggle, environmentalism, and the ethics of expansion with genuine nuance—there are no easy answers. The romance between Hachimaki and Tanabe develops organically over 26 episodes, earning every emotional beat. Planetes proves that anime can tackle big ideas without sacrificing humanity or entertainment value. It's a travesty that this isn't mentioned alongside the medium's greatest achievements.

6. March Comes in Like a Lion

Studio Shaft's adaptation of this shogi manga could have been a niche sports anime. Instead, it became one of the most sensitive portrayals of depression, trauma, and healing in any medium. Rei Kiriyama is a professional shogi player who lives alone, estranged from his adoptive family and struggling with survivor's guilt after losing his parents and sister in an accident. His life changes when he's taken in by the Kawamoto sisters, whose warmth gradually helps him rediscover human connection.

Director Akiyuki Shinbo employs his full visual arsenal—stark color palettes during Rei's depressive episodes, warm golden tones in the Kawamoto household, abstract imagery to represent internal states. The show doesn't romanticize mental illness or offer easy fixes. Recovery is portrayed as gradual, non-linear, with setbacks and small victories. The shogi matches are tense and well-executed, but they're ultimately metaphors for Rei's internal battles. The supporting cast is richly developed, particularly the Kawamoto sisters who each have their own struggles and growth arcs. This is a quiet, contemplative series that trusts its audience to engage with difficult emotions. It's a shame that its deliberate pacing kept it from finding a wider audience.

5. Monster

Naoki Urasawa's psychological thriller about a doctor hunting a former patient turned serial killer should be mentioned alongside Death Note and Steins;Gate, yet it remains a cult favorite rather than a mainstream classic. Dr. Kenzo Tenma saves a young boy's life, only to discover years later that his patient became a charismatic monster responsible for countless deaths. The series follows Tenma across Europe as he attempts to correct his mistake while questioning the nature of good, evil, and moral responsibility.

Monster's 74-episode runtime intimidates potential viewers, but the length serves the story. This is a slow-burn thriller that meticulously builds atmosphere, develops a massive cast of interconnected characters, and explores the legacy of Cold War trauma on Eastern Europe. Johan Liebert ranks among anime's greatest villains—not through supernatural powers but through psychological manipulation and philosophical nihilism. The show raises genuine ethical questions about whether some lives are worth saving, whether evil can be innate, and what justice actually means. The production values are dated, but the storytelling remains timeless. Monster deserves to be considered essential anime, not a hidden gem.

4. Kyousougiga

Explaining Kyousougiga feels impossible because the show operates on dream logic and emotional resonance rather than conventional narrative structure. A monk creates a pocket dimension called Mirror Kyoto where he builds a family with a reincarnated goddess and three adopted children. When he disappears, the family fractures. Decades later, his daughter Koto arrives in Mirror Kyoto searching for her parents, triggering a chain of events that could destroy or save the dimension.

The plot summary doesn't capture what makes Kyousougiga special. This is experimental anime in the best sense—visually explosive, thematically rich, emotionally vulnerable. The animation is gorgeously kinetic, with reality-bending action sequences and jaw-dropping sakuga. Beneath the chaos is a deeply moving story about found family, the pain of abandonment, and learning to communicate love across generational divides. Each family member gets developed with care, their quirks and traumas explored through surreal imagery and Buddhist symbolism. The finale brings everything together in a cathartic, earned emotional climax. Kyousougiga was too weird for mainstream success, but it's a vibrant, heartfelt masterpiece that deserves cult status at minimum.

3. Texhnolyze

This is not an easy watch. Texhnolyze is aggressively bleak, deliberately paced, and philosophically nihilistic. The first episode contains almost no dialogue. Characters are introduced without explanation. The underground city of Lux feels oppressive and hopeless by design. Many viewers bounce off it within three episodes. Those who persevere experience one of anime's most uncompromising visions—a meditation on civilization's decay, technological dehumanization, and the human will to survive even when survival seems pointless.

Texhnolyze follows Ichise, a boxer who loses limbs in a gang conflict and receives experimental prosthetic replacements. But this isn't a revenge story or an action thriller. It's a descent into existential horror as various factions battle for control of a dying city while the surface world plots their extinction. The show explores transhumanism's darker implications—what happens when the boundary between human and machine erodes, when evolution becomes forced, when hope itself becomes a liability. The animation and sound design create an atmosphere of industrial decay and psychological dread. The ending is devastatingly appropriate. Texhnolyze isn't entertainment in the traditional sense; it's an experience that lingers uncomfortably. It will never be popular, but it demands to be recognized as a singular artistic achievement.

2. The Tatami Galaxy

Masaaki Yuasa's hyperactive masterpiece feels like it was designed to filter out casual viewers. The dialogue moves at breakneck speed. The art style is aggressively unconventional. The protagonist is a bitter, self-sabotaging misanthrope who spends most of the series blaming others for his failures. And yet, beneath the manic energy and experimental presentation is one of anime's most insightful examinations of regret, choice, and the paralysis of believing in a "perfect" life path.

Each episode resets to the protagonist's first day of university, exploring an alternate timeline where he joins a different club and makes different choices. The structure initially seems like a gimmick until you realize the show is systematically deconstructing the notion that any single choice determines your worth. The protagonist keeps chasing an idealized "rose-colored campus life," failing to recognize that his own self-destructive patterns follow him across every timeline. The finale delivers a profound message about accepting imperfection and finding meaning in the life you've actually lived rather than the one you imagined. Tatami Galaxy's visual experimentation and narrative structure scared off many viewers, but it remains one of the smartest, most rewarding anime ever produced.

1. Serial Experiments Lain

In 1998, Serial Experiments Lain predicted internet culture with eerie accuracy—social isolation through digital connection, the erosion of consensus reality, online identity replacing physical selfhood, and the psychological toll of information overload. Watching it today feels like viewing a prophetic text that understood where technology was heading before most people owned a computer.

The series follows Lain Iwakura, a socially withdrawn girl who receives an email from a classmate who recently committed suicide, claiming she's abandoned her body to live in the Wired (the show's version of the internet). As Lain investigates, the boundary between the physical world and the Wired dissolves, reality becomes malleable, and Lain discovers she may not be entirely human. The show is deliberately opaque, layered with symbolism drawn from psychology, philosophy, and cyberpunk theory. It demands interpretation rather than passive consumption.

What makes Lain essential is its willingness to be genuinely experimental while maintaining emotional coherence. Lain's loneliness is palpable. Her search for identity and connection feels achingly human even as the show ventures into abstract territory. The atmosphere is unsettling—low hums, electronic drones, and oppressive silence create constant unease. Some episodes are nearly incomprehensible on first viewing, but the show rewards re-watches and analysis. Serial Experiments Lain influenced everything from The Matrix to Paprika to modern isekai, yet it remains underseen compared to its cultural impact. It's not just underrated—it's foundational anime that should be required viewing for anyone serious about the medium.

Final Thoughts

The common thread connecting these series is ambition—whether narrative, thematic, or stylistic. They refused to play it safe, refused to pander, and often paid the price in popularity. Some were too slow. Others were too weird. A few demanded too much intellectual engagement from audiences trained on more conventional fare. But every single entry represents artists working at the height of their craft, creating something meaningful that transcends mere entertainment.

Underrated doesn't mean perfect. Several of these shows have flaws—pacing issues, dated animation, occasionally impenetrable symbolism. But they all share a commitment to treating the audience as intelligent, to exploring complex ideas with nuance, and to using anime's unique strengths as a visual medium. They prove that commercial success and artistic merit aren't synonymous, and that sometimes the most rewarding viewing experiences come from taking chances on unfamiliar titles.

If you've exhausted the standard recommendation lists and crave something different, start here. These anime won't all resonate with everyone—some are deliberately challenging, others are acquired tastes. But each one offers something you won't find in more popular series: a unique vision executed with conviction, artistry that respects your intelligence, and stories that linger long after the credits roll. They deserve more than cult status. They deserve to be part of the conversation about what makes anime great.
